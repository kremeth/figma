Confounded metrics

Recovery

Three things pulled Recovery down independently of the supplements: a caloric deficit (90 → 88 kg in week one), a 90-minute bedtime drift (midnight → 1:30am), and one alcohol event on Apr 10 that crashed a single night to 4 and dragged the weekly average with it. With that much behavioral load, a flat Recovery score tells us nothing — it could mean the supplements aren’t working, or they could be working and the headwinds are cancelling them out. A cleaner window is needed.

HRV

HRV went from 46 ms to 44 ms post-stack — but the caloric deficit alone suppresses HRV by 5–15%. Add the alcohol event (drops HRV 20–40% for 24–48 hours), the caffeine taper from 4+ cups to 1–2 (real HRV noise for 2 weeks while your system adjusts), and the bedtime drift, and there’s more than enough behavioral signal to explain every millisecond of that drop. This isn’t a supplement non-response. It’s a noisy window.

Sleep Quality

Subjective sleep quality dropped from “fairly good” to “fairly bad” between Mar 30 and Apr 13, and sleep latency doubled from 16–30 min to 31–60 min — tracking the weight loss (90 → 88 kg) almost exactly. Caloric deficits reliably worsen subjective sleep quality and increase time-to-fall-asleep, independent of any supplement. The 90-minute bedtime drift and intermittent sleep medication add more noise on top. We can’t separate “magnesium isn’t helping” from “magnesium is helping but the deficit is winning” until the behavioral noise clears.

Workout Effort

Training intensity fell from 48% to 37% and compound lifts dropped across the board — but three variables explain it before CoQ10 enters the picture. A caloric deficit reduces glycogen availability; the lift pattern is the textbook deficit signature. Cutting caffeine from 4+ cups to 1–2 cups simultaneously removed one of the most effective performance enhancers known. And the Apr 10 alcohol event plus a 160-min training week (vs 441 min the week prior) dragged the average down further. Training effort genuinely dropped. CoQ10 didn’t cause it.

VO2 Max

WHOOP doesn’t measure VO2 max directly — it calculates it from HRV and resting heart rate. Every confound affecting HRV (caloric deficit, caffeine taper, bedtime drift, alcohol) flows straight into the VO2 max number, even if actual aerobic fitness hasn’t changed. There’s also a structural issue: CoQ10 improves mitochondrial efficiency during aerobic training, but your training is entirely weightlifting and walking. There’s no aerobic stimulus for it to work on. A derived metric that inherits every HRV confound, with no aerobic training in the window — this cell is unreadable.

Don’t add

Berberine

Stacking a metabolic agent on top of an aggressive deficit risks too much catabolic signal at once.

B12

Daily beef, chicken, and eggs means you’re as B12-replete as a 40-year-old can realistically be. Supplementing B12 at normal serum levels is inert — the body excretes the excess. Not harmful, just pointless.

Vitamin E

Your diet already covers Vitamin E — olive oil, nuts, and seeds are in your usual foods and are the premier dietary sources. Omega-3 already covers the oxidative-stress and cardiovascular angles.

Add

Ashwagandha

Directly addresses your biggest unresolved problem: elevated cortisol from the caloric deficit, which is dragging down HRV, recovery, and workout effort simultaneously.

Probiotics

Your diet is structurally gut-unfriendly — no legumes, oats, or quinoa, limited fermented foods, heavy on beef and wheat. The gut-brain axis feeds directly into HRV, sleep, and stress response through vagal tone and serotonin precursor availability.

L-Theanine

Targets the one metric that clearly worsened: sleep latency went from 16–30 min to 31–60 min. Highest evidence score in the library for sleep onset (25.2 across 4 papers). The mechanism — alpha-wave promotion, glutamate/GABA modulation — is non-sedating, which pairs well with your reduced caffeine intake.

Zinc

Zinc hits exactly where your sleep is weakest — latency, REM, and deep sleep — the same areas your check-ins flagged as worsening. It also protects testosterone during your current caloric deficit, which nothing else in your stack covers.

CoQ10

Confounded

CoQ10’s targets — workout effort, recovery, VO2 max — all dropped or stayed flat. But each movement is fully explained without CoQ10: workout effort dropped from caffeine reduction and glycogen depletion; recovery stayed flat because deficits suppress it directly; VO2 max is derived from HRV, so it inherits every HRV confound. At day 23, CoQ10 is also still inside its 2–4 week effect window.

Omega-3

Too early

HRV, RHR, sleep disruptions, and REM are flat or confounded — but EPA/DHA membrane incorporation takes 4–8 weeks. At day 23, you’re still 2+ weeks short of the earliest expected effect window. No signal here is consistent with expected timing, not a failure.

Best read: the supplement’s biological window hasn’t opened yet. Evaluate properly at day 60 when plasma EPA/DHA has reached steady state.

Review at day 90

Vitamin D

Too early

The slowest supplement in the stack — serum 25(OH)D takes 6–12 weeks to rise meaningfully. At day 23, you’re 3+ weeks short of the earliest effect window. One confound actually strengthens the case: sun exposure dropped from 5+ days/week to 1–2 days, shifting you from making endogenous vitamin D to being almost entirely dependent on the supplement. The reduced sun makes this more important, not less.

Best read: even earlier in its window than omega-3, and the sun-exposure drop makes keeping it more justified. Evaluate at day 90 with a serum 25(OH)D blood test for a real read.

Bottom line

One clear win, three waiting on time, four more on the way.

The first 23 days ran through a caloric deficit, a caffeine taper, a 90-minute bedtime drift, and one alcohol event that distorted your HRV and Recovery averages on its own. One clean win survived all of that: sleep disruptions dropped from 8 to 6 per night — exactly what Magnesium is supposed to do.

Everything else is either too early or too confounded to read. Omega-3 and Vitamin D haven’t reached their biological windows (4–8 weeks and 6–12 weeks respectively) — a flat reading isn’t a miss, it’s measurement before the mechanism can act. CoQ10’s targets — HRV, VO2 max, workout effort — are all suppressed by the deficit and behavioral load. That’s a noise problem, not a supplement problem.

The four additions fill the gaps the current stack misses. Ashwagandha targets the cortisol axis inflamed by the caloric deficit. Probiotics support the gut-brain pathway your low-fiber, high-meat diet leaves underserved. L-Theanine directly addresses the sleep latency that worsened in your check-ins. Zinc reinforces sleep architecture and protects testosterone during fat loss.

It’s always important to remember that supplements can only do so much on their own. If your metrics move once lifestyle factors stabilise, the stack is doing its job. The lifestyle side is always the first and most important place to look. But you don’t have to figure any of this out alone — we’ll keep watching the data with you, month after month, and adjust where it actually needs adjusting.
